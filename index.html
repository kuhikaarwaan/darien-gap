For decades, the Darién Gap was crossed by only a handful of adventurers each year. Today, it has become the deadliest corridor in the Western Hemisphere's migration crisis. In 2023 alone, more than 520,000 people — including more than 113,000 children — made this dangerous journey, more than double the previous year's record.

They come from Venezuela, fleeing economic collapse. From Haiti, escaping gang violence. From Ecuador, China, Afghanistan, and dozens of other countries. All share the same destination: the United States, and the promise of something better.

The Darién Gap

A 60-mile stretch of roadless jungle separates Colombia and Panama — the only break in the Pan-American Highway that otherwise connects Alaska to Argentina. There is no route for cars, buses, or rail lines. Only smugglers' footpaths twist through the rainforest.

Every crossing begins with this knowledge: once the pavement ends, there is no official infrastructure until you emerge on the Panamanian side. Everything in between belongs to the jungle and the groups that control it.

The five-to-fifteen-day trek

The trek takes migrants through dense rainforest, steep mountains, and fast-flowing rivers. Temperatures reach 95°F with suffocating humidity. Crocodiles stalk the banks, venomous snakes hide beneath roots, and flash floods arrive without warning.

Criminal gangs charge tolls or rob entire caravans. Each night in the jungle carries the risk of injury, extortion, or disappearance for the families making the crossing.

The Exponential Rise

Migrants are crossing the Darién in record numbers. After a brief pause during the pandemic, unauthorized crossings through Panama exploded. Fewer than 9,000 people crossed in 2018; by 2023 the total hit 520,085 — an almost six-fold jump in just two years and a global record for this route. The spike eased somewhat in 2024 when Panama’s migration service logged 302,203 crossings, down 42% from the prior year.

The 2023 surge had many drivers. Changing migration policies across Latin America left few legal routes. The U.S. rescinded Title 42 at the start of 2023, ending a pandemic-era rule that quickly expelled many asylum-seekers at the border. Even as asylum grew harder to secure, word spread that the end of Title 42 opened a brief window while a tripartite U.S.-Colombia-Panama plan was still forming.

Who Is Crossing?

Panama’s National Migration Service keeps annual totals of those who make it through. A chart of yearly crossings from 2018–2024 shows the jagged climb: 8,445 in 2018, 221,102 in 2019, a dip to 8,594 in 2020 (during COVID), then 133,653 in 2021, 248,784 in 2022, 520,085 in 2023, and 302,203 in 2024. These public figures were confirmed by Panama’s migration authorities and by independent monitors.

This trend shows how politics and crisis drive the flow. A wave of Venezuelan migrants arrived after 2021, fueling rapid growth. Panama’s new government, in office since mid-2024, built fences and imposed fines to slow crossings. They deported some who crossed illegally yet allowed tens of thousands from troubled countries to continue north.

Rising Sexual Violence

Aid groups and government records both show hundreds of reported rapes and assaults, though experts say the true number is higher. Médecins Sans Frontières (MSF) runs clinics near the gap and has treated dozens of survivors, warning that cruelty has escalated sharply. Official statistics and MSF case data together paint a disturbing picture.

The chart should compare reported sexual assaults vs. clinical cases from 2018–2024. In 2023, Panama recorded only 69 cases of sexual violence on the route, yet MSF treated 462 survivors by November. In 2022, the official count was 91 while MSF treated 232 people. Survivors often avoid police — they want to keep moving, fear retaliation, or are too traumatized — so aid clinics see many more cases.

The Missing and the Dead

IOM’s Missing Migrants Project tracks fatalities here. Deaths spiked in 2024 even as crossings dipped slightly. A bar chart of fatalities by year would highlight recent jumps: 2024 saw 174 documented deaths or disappearances, surpassing the previous 146 in 2022. Lower figures in 2020–2021 reflect fewer migrants — and 2024’s tally only covers January through August.

One heartbreaking statistic is the number of children who die en route. Data records 22 child fatalities in 2024 alone, up from 15 in 2022 and 14 in 2023. All of the deaths recorded in 2024 were among children under age five.

Root Causes and the American Dream

The numbers above — crossing totals, nationalities, sexual assaults, and fatalities — all reflect deeper push and pull factors. Migrants in the Darién Gap flee crises or chase opportunity. Nearly all come from countries suffering violence, poverty, or natural disaster. Venezuelans cite hyperinflation and food shortages; Haitians cite gang warfare and political collapse; migrants from Asia and Africa cite human rights abuses or conflict at home.

On the “pull” side, many migrants are driven by hope for a better life in the United States — the so-called American dream. Migrant shelters near the Panama–Costa Rica border post information about U.S. asylum policies. The spike in 2023 was partly attributed to rumors that the U.S. quietly relaxed entry conditions after Title 42 ended. Hope travels faster than official policy.

Many migrants arrive in Panama saying they walk for the American dream.
